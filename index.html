Keynesian Economics

Keynesian economics states that economic output is strongly influenced by the total amount of demand in an economy. As such, Keynesian economists generally recommend that during recessions, the government should decrease taxes and increase spending in order to generate greater total demand.

Phillips Curve

The Phillips Curve is a curve demonstrating the fact that higher rates of inflation are correlated with lower rates of unemployment. The idea is that if unemployment becomes lower, then the employees gain more bargaining power (as the employers must compete harder for employees). This bargaining power results in higher wages, causing inflation.

Classical Economics

Classical economics is an economic school of thought based off of Enlightenment-era philosophes such as Adam Smith or David Ricardo. Classical economics views the economy as a self-regulating system governed by the "Invisible Hand," or a set of scientific/mathematical laws that affect supply, demand, price, and more. Classical economists typically advocate for a free market.

Pareto Principle

The Pareto Principle is the idea that approximately 20% of a given X will be responsible for 80% of a given Y. Here we see it applied to wealth in the United States. A small percentage of all people in the United States possess the majority of the wealth.

Comparative Advantage

Comparative advantage is the principle that states that in a system of free trade (no tariffs or trade restrictions) that a country will naturally specialize in what they are more efficient in producing.

To see why this is the case, imagine that our country (A) can produce one pen per hour, but can produce two pencils in one hour. We are more efficient at producing pencils than we are at pens. Now imagine that our neighboring country (B) can produce two pens in one hour, but only one pencil in an hour.

Let's say that these two countries are willing to trade at a rate of one pen for one pencil.

If our country trades one pencil for one pen, this is a great deal for us because normally in order to obtain one pen, we would be sacrificing one hour, and therefore the ability to produce two pencils in that time.

If the other country trades one pen for one pencil, this is a great deal for them as well because normally in order to obtain one pencil, they would need to sacrifice one hour, and therefore the ability to produce two pens in that time.

Supply-Side Economics

Supply-side economics views the economy as primarily driven by supply rather than demand. They advocate for a reduction of taxes and typically a free market approach.

Laffer Curve

The Laffer Curve states that as the government taxes more, the revenue gained from taxation will gradually go up as taxes increase. After a certain point, the revenue will go back down because the excessive taxation is harming the economy.

The following extremely simplified example shows the revenue gained from taxing a shop that sells sandwiches for $10 each. As the taxation increases, the store sells less sandwiches, causing the revenue to decrease sharply after the tax rate reaches 20%.
